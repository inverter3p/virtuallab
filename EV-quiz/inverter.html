2-Level Inverter with Sinusoidal PWM (SPWM)

SPWM Limitation

Peak Line-to-Line voltage is limited.

VL-L, peak = 0.866 × VDC

This means the full potential of the high-voltage battery is not utilized.

A 2-level inverter using Sinusoidal Pulse Width Modulation (SPWM) generates a waveform where the peak Line-to-Line voltage is limited to 86.6% of the DC bus voltage (VDC). This means that the full potential of the high-voltage battery is not utilized.

2-Level Inverter with Space Vector Modulation (SVM)

SVM Advantage

Adds a 3rd harmonic to boost output.

VL-L, peak = 1.000 × VDC

Results in a ~15.5% increase in output voltage and full utilization of the battery's VDC rating.

By using Space Vector Modulation (SVM), the inverter can generate a waveform that includes a 3rd harmonic component. In a balanced three-phase system, this harmonic is cancelled out, which allows for a higher fundamental output voltage. This method enables the peak Line-to-Line voltage to reach 100% of the VDC, resulting in a ~15.5% increase in output voltage compared to SPWM.

Vector Control of PMSM

Field-Oriented Control (FOC)

Creates a rotating magnetic field.

Representations:

  • ABC (stator)
  • αβ (stationary)

Field-Oriented Control (FOC) is a method used for the vector control of Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motors (PMSM). It involves creating a rotating magnetic field, which can be represented by both ABC and αβ space vectors. This control strategy is crucial for achieving high performance in PMSM drives.

Space Vector Modulation (SVM)

SVM Hexagon

Switching configurations (S1, S3, S5) create specific voltage vectors.

[Diagram of the Space Vector Hexagon]

SVM is a technique where different switching configurations of the inverter's power devices (S1, S3, S5) create specific voltage vectors. These vectors determine the magnitude and direction of the voltage applied to the motor terminals, which can be visualized in a space vector hexagon. This allows for precise control over the motor's operation.

Space Vector Modulation (SVM) Algorithm

Algorithm Steps

  1. Determine voltage sector.
  2. Calculate "ON" time for sequence.
  3. Generate gate pulse signals (via MCU).

The SVM algorithm consists of several key steps. First, the voltage sector is determined. Then, the "ON" time for the switching sequence in each sector is calculated. This involves using trigonometry to solve for coefficients that determine the magnitude of two adjacent space vectors. Finally, this information is used to generate the gate pulse signals for the inverter's switches, which can be implemented using a Microcontroller Unit (MCU).

Losses in Power Devices

Two Main Types of Loss

1. Conduction Loss:

Occurs when device is "on".

2. Switching Loss:

Occurs during "on" ↔ "off" transition.

Power devices in inverters experience two main types of losses: conduction loss and switching loss. Conduction loss occurs when the device is in the "on" state and conducting current. Switching loss occurs during the transition between the "on" and "off" states. These losses contribute to the overall inefficiency of the inverter and generate heat.

Effect of Losses in Power Devices

Consequences of Losses

  • Reduced inverter efficiency.
  • Temperature rise (heat).
  • Thermal cycling ⇒ degrades module lifecycle.

The losses in power devices have a direct impact on the inverter's efficiency and lead to a temperature rise. This thermal cycling can degrade the lifecycle of the power modules. Therefore, managing these losses is crucial for the longevity and reliability of the inverter.

Switching Frequency Considerations

Trade-offs

Higher Frequency ↑

  • Better output waveform quality
  • Increased switching losses
  • Increased EMI (Interference)

The choice of switching frequency in an inverter involves a trade-off between several factors. A higher switching frequency can improve the quality of the output waveform but also increases switching losses. Additionally, it can lead to electromagnetic interference (EMI) issues that need to be addressed in the design.

Core Terminology

The Language of Batteries

  • Physical: Cell Type, Form Factor, Module, Pack
  • Chemical: Anode, Cathode, Electrolyte, Separator
  • Electrical: Capacity (Ah), Energy Density (Wh/kg), Power Density (W/kg), C-Rate, Cycle Life, BMS

To understand EV batteries, we first need to learn the language. The terminology can be grouped into three categories. Physical terms describe the construction, from the smallest 'cell' to the 'module' and finally the 'pack'. Chemical terms refer to the internal components. Electrical terms describe its performance—how much energy it stores, how fast it can be charged/discharged, and how long it lasts.

Inside a Li-ion Cell

Key Components

  • Cathode (+): Stores lithium when discharged. Material (NMC, LFP) determines capacity/voltage.
  • Anode (-): Stores lithium when charged. Typically graphite.
  • Electrolyte: Liquid medium for ion flow.
  • Separator: Physical barrier to prevent short circuits.

Every Li-ion cell contains four key components. The Cathode and Anode store the lithium. The Electrolyte is the transport medium for ions. The Separator is a critical safety component that allows ions to pass but keeps the electrodes from touching. During discharge (powering the car), lithium ions travel from the anode to the cathode. During charging, the process is reversed.

Types of Li-ion Chemistries

Balancing the Trade-offs

  • NMC (Nickel Manganese Cobalt): Good balance of high energy density and decent cycle life.
  • LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate): Prioritizes safety, long cycle life, and low cost (no cobalt). Lower energy density.
  • NCA (Nickel Cobalt Aluminum): High energy and long life, but more expensive and higher safety concerns.

There is no "best" battery chemistry; each is a compromise. The cathode material is key. NMC is a common all-rounder. LFP is popular for standard-range vehicles where its safety, long life, and low cost are advantages. NCA is a high-performance option, famously used by Tesla, that pushes for maximum energy density.

Cell Form Factors

Shape and Structure

  • Cylindrical: (e.g., 18650, 4680). Excellent thermal performance. Used by Tesla.
  • Prismatic: Rectangular, can-like shape. Space-efficient for packing.
  • Pouch: Flexible, soft case. Best packaging efficiency but needs structural support.

Battery cells are made in three primary shapes. Cylindrical cells are robust and easy to manufacture. Prismatic cells are hard-cased rectangles that stack easily. Pouch cells are like a sealed foil bag, offering design flexibility, but they need to be protected and constrained within the pack structure to prevent swelling.

Key Performance Factors

The Five Pillars

  • Energy Density (Wh/kg): Determines vehicle range.
  • Power Density (W/kg): Affects acceleration.
  • Cycle Life: How many times it can be charged/discharged.
  • Safety: Thermal stability and risk of fire.
  • Cost ($/kWh): The single biggest factor in the price of an EV.

When evaluating an EV battery, we look at five key factors. Energy density dictates range. Power density affects acceleration. Cycle life determines lifespan. Safety focuses on preventing thermal runaway. Cost remains a critical barrier to EV adoption.

From Cell to Pack: The Tesla Model S Example

Building a Battery Pack

Cell: A single 18650 cell (~3.7V, ~3.4Ah)

Module (6S 74P):

  • 6 cells in Series (Voltage ↑: 6 * 3.7V ≈ 22.2V)
  • 74 cells in Parallel (Capacity ↑: 74 * 3.4Ah ≈ 251.6Ah)

Pack: 16 modules in Series (16 * 22.2V ≈ 355V).

An EV pack is an assembly of thousands of cells. In the classic Tesla Model S, cells are first connected in parallel to increase capacity (a "brick"). Bricks are then connected in series to increase voltage. 74 cells in parallel (74P) are connected to 6 such groups in series (6S) to form one module. Finally, 16 modules are connected in series to create the high-voltage pack.

Battery Management System (BMS)

The Brain of the Battery

  • Protection: Prevents over-charge, over-discharge, over-current, and over-temperature.
  • Monitoring: Calculates State of Charge (SoC) and State of Health (SoH).
  • Cell Balancing: Ensures all cells in series have an equal voltage.
  • Communication: Reports battery status to the rest of the vehicle.

The Battery Management System (BMS) is an electronic brain supervising the pack. Its most critical job is protection, keeping cells within safe limits. It monitors voltage and temperature to estimate the "fuel level" (SoC) and lifespan (SoH). A key function is cell balancing, which ensures all cells are at the same voltage to maximize capacity. Without a BMS, a large Li-ion pack would be unsafe and have a short life.

Thermal Management

Keeping it Cool (and Warm)

Problem: Li-ion batteries are sensitive. Too hot, they degrade and risk thermal runaway. Too cold, performance drops.

Solution: Active thermal management. Most EVs use liquid cooling/heating with coolant circulated through channels in contact with the cells.

Control: Aims to keep the maximum cell temperature in the ideal range (e.g., 15-35°C).

Maintaining the battery's optimal temperature is critical for performance, safety, and longevity. Batteries generate heat during fast charging and high-power discharge. To manage this, EVs use a Thermal Management System (TMS), typically circulating liquid coolant to draw away heat. The same system can warm the batteries in cold weather. The system is controlled based on the hottest cell, not the average, to ensure no single cell overheats.

Charge Capacity & C-Rate

Measuring Capacity and Speed

Capacity (Ah): Amp-hours. A measure of charge. (e.g., 100 Ah = 100A for 1hr, or 10A for 10hrs).

C-Rate: A normalized measure of charge/discharge rate relative to capacity.

  • 1C = Charge/discharge in 1 hour.
  • 0.5C = Charge/discharge in 2 hours.
  • 2C = Charge/discharge in 30 minutes.

A battery's charge capacity is measured in Amp-hours (Ah). This tells us how much current it can provide over time. To talk about charging speed independent of size, we use the C-rate. A 1C rate means delivering a current equal to the Ah capacity, discharging it in one hour. A 2C rate means twice the current, discharging in half an hour. This is crucial for understanding fast charging specs.

State of Charge (SoC) & Depth of Discharge (DoD)

The Battery's Fuel Gauge

State of Charge (SoC): The current charge level as a percentage of max capacity. 100% is full.

Depth of Discharge (DoD): The percentage of capacity discharged. It's the inverse of SoC.

SoC% + DoD% = 100%

Impact: Cycle life is strongly affected by DoD. Smaller discharges lead to a longer life.

State of Charge (SoC) is the equivalent of a fuel gauge, expressed as a percentage. Depth of Discharge (DoD) is the opposite; it's the percentage used. If SoC is 70%, DoD is 30%. This matters because the battery's lifespan (cycle life) is heavily dependent on how deeply it is discharged on average. To prolong life, it's best to avoid regular deep discharges to very low SoC levels.

Charging Characteristics

The CC-CV Method

Li-ion batteries use a two-stage charging profile:

Stage 1: Constant Current (CC)
The charger supplies a high, steady current. Voltage rises. This is the fast part of charging (e.g., up to ~80% SoC).

Stage 2: Constant Voltage (CV)
Once the battery hits its max voltage (e.g., 4.2V), the charger holds that voltage. The current tapers off as the battery becomes full.

Charging a Li-ion battery uses a specific two-stage process called Constant Current, Constant Voltage (CC-CV). In the first stage (CC), the charger provides a steady current, allowing the battery to absorb energy quickly. This continues until the cell voltage hits its upper limit. At that point, the charger switches to the second stage (CV), holding the voltage at that maximum level. The current will then gradually decrease as the battery "tops off". This is why charging from 80% to 100% takes much longer than from 20% to 40%.

Self-Discharge

Losing Charge Over Time

  • A natural, internal chemical process, not a manufacturing defect.
  • The rate of discharge increases significantly with higher temperatures.
  • A higher State of Charge (SoC) also leads to a higher rate of self-discharge.

All batteries naturally lose some of their charge over time, even when not connected to anything. This is called self-discharge. It's an inherent characteristic of the battery's chemistry. The rate of this discharge is highly dependent on two main factors: temperature and state of charge. A battery stored in a hot environment will lose its charge much faster than one in a cool place. Similarly, a battery stored at 100% SoC will lose charge faster than one stored at 50% SoC, which is why long-term storage recommendations often specify a partial charge.

Cycle Life

Measuring Battery Lifespan

Definition of a "Cycle": A cycle is counted each time the accumulated discharge capacity equals the battery's total design capacity.

This means a cycle is not just one 0-100% discharge. Two discharges from 80% to 30% (50% DoD each) would equal one full equivalent cycle.

Degradation: Battery capacity naturally decreases with each cycle. Cycle life is often defined as the number of cycles until the capacity drops to 80% of its original value.

A battery's cycle life is a measure of its longevity. A "cycle" is not simply one instance of charging and discharging. It's a cumulative measure. One cycle is completed every time the total energy discharged adds up to the battery's nominal capacity. For example, discharging a battery by 25% and recharging it four times is equivalent to one full cycle. As a battery ages and undergoes more cycles, its ability to hold a charge (its capacity) gradually decreases. A manufacturer's warranty often specifies the cycle life until the battery's capacity degrades to a certain point, typically 70-80% of its initial capacity.

Battery Modeling

Simulating a Battery

Why Model? To predict battery behavior (voltage, temp, SoC) for BMS algorithm development without needing a physical battery.

Equivalent Circuit Models (ECM): A common approach using ideal voltage sources, resistors, and capacitors to simulate a real battery.

Thevenin 2-RC Model: A popular ECM that captures internal resistance (`Rs`) and transient voltage effects (`R1/C1`, `R2/C2`).

To develop the complex algorithms inside a BMS, engineers need to simulate how a battery will behave. This is done through battery modeling. A popular method is the Equivalent Circuit Model (ECM), which represents the battery's complex chemistry with a simple circuit of resistors and capacitors. For example, a resistor `Rs` models the immediate voltage drop when current is drawn, while RC pairs model slower voltage changes. These models are crucial for accurately estimating a battery's State of Charge in real-time, as their parameters change with temperature and SoC.
